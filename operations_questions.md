# HAAG Engagement Initiative — Operations Questions

This document answers the operations-focused reflection questions for the HAAG Engagement Initiative. It is grounded in the initiative’s purpose of creating sustainable, repeatable systems for external engagement and collaboration support.

## Define what reoccurring tasks you are doing within your project group.

The recurring tasks in this project group are primarily coordination and documentation tasks rather than technical implementation tasks tied to a single research system. These recurring tasks include reviewing the current state of HAAG website and stakeholder-facing materials, documenting existing engagement processes, identifying missing or outdated information, drafting and revising procedures, organizing deliverables for each workstream, and aligning stakeholders around what the engagement system should look like.

There is also a recurring management layer that includes following up on open documentation items, checking whether proposed workflows are specific enough to be executed by someone new, and maintaining a consistent structure across artifacts so the initiative remains usable over time. As the project matures, recurring tasks should also include reviewing incoming stakeholder interactions, tracking update requests, and validating whether the published procedures still match actual practice.

## How are you assessing your role as the project manager and its effect on the overall project?

My role as project manager is being assessed through the degree of structure, clarity, and momentum that the initiative now has. In a project like this, success is not just about producing documents. It is about making sure people know what they are responsible for, what needs to be built next, and how the work connects to measurable outcomes.

The most important indicator of whether the role is effective is whether the group is moving from broad ideas to executable systems. If procedures are becoming clearer, deliverables are being defined, and stakeholders understand what decisions still need to be made, then the project management role is having a positive effect. If work remains vague or highly dependent on one person’s informal knowledge, that is a sign that project management still needs to create more structure.

## What challenges and success have you observed in your group?

One success is that the group has already framed the initiative in a strong organizational way. The project clearly distinguishes between website engagement and stakeholder engagement, which helps break a broad problem into manageable workstreams. Another success is that the initiative already includes deliverables and evaluation criteria, which gives the group a concrete basis for planning and review.

One challenge is that the project depends heavily on converting conceptual goals into practical workflows. It is easier to agree that HAAG should improve collaboration pathways than it is to define exactly who updates content, who receives inquiries, and how handoffs are documented. Another challenge is sustaining momentum when the work is process-heavy and its value may not be immediately visible unless the group actively tests the procedures.

## How are you collaborating with stakeholders to ensure your role is supporting and motivating the group?

I am collaborating with stakeholders by translating broad engagement goals into clearer procedures, expected artifacts, and measurable checkpoints. This supports the group because it reduces ambiguity and helps people see how their contributions fit into the initiative as a whole. It also motivates the group because progress becomes more visible when the work is broken into specific deliverables rather than left at the level of general discussion.

This role also involves listening for friction points. If administrators need clearer ownership definitions, if contributors need simpler templates, or if external-facing materials are too vague, then part of supporting the group is adjusting the process so that the initiative becomes easier to participate in. Motivation improves when people feel that the workflow is realistic and their effort leads to an outcome that others will actually use.

## What plans do you have for enhancing your role as the project manager with the remaining time in the semester?

With the remaining time in the semester, the focus should be on moving from planning into validation. That means prioritizing completion of the highest-value documents, piloting at least one website workflow and one stakeholder engagement workflow, and collecting evidence about where the process still breaks down.

It would also be useful to strengthen the project manager role by creating a lightweight operating cadence, such as weekly artifact reviews, ownership checks on open documentation, and a simple tracking dashboard for initiative status. Another enhancement would be to prepare a handoff package so the next person managing the initiative can continue the work without losing context.

## Please provide feedback on what support you may need in your role.

The most important support needed in this role is stakeholder responsiveness and clarity of authority. Because this initiative crosses organizational boundaries, progress depends on being able to get timely input on website practices, research visibility, and stakeholder communication norms. Without that, documentation risks becoming detached from actual group behavior.

Additional support that would help includes clearer agreement on document ownership, access to the current systems that need to be analyzed, opportunities to test workflows with real users, and feedback from faculty advisors or administrators on what a successful engagement system should prioritize first. Support is also needed in the form of timely review, because a process initiative only becomes useful when drafts are actively challenged, refined, and adopted.
